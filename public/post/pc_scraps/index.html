By Nathan Cheever

So far, Political Correctness is like other types of correctness in that compliance is key. In particular, compliance in speech. But here again, political correctness cannot mean what I thought it did, because America’s Constitution declares freedom of speech a basic right of all citizens. So how could PC mean compliance to the constitution when that standard seeks not to curb free speech but to protect it as a basic right?

But not all incorrectness is equally weighted. Miscalculating 7 x 7 for 42 carries no moral weight, but being called out for political incorrectness seems to. And yet, ironically, the more established the standard, as are the multiplication tables, the less moral guilt is experienced. Yet with a vague and uncertain standard, how scalding and certain the denunciation! In this case, I don’t know whether to decry the inequality or celebrate the diversity!

Any morality worth its salt needs to persuade me to believe in it, and I’m persuadable! But any thoughtless bully can twist your arm and make you yell “Uncle!” or “First Nation Peoples!”

Any society that thinks it can do better without them loses itself, for without them, are we even human?

A less-well known French Philosopher and playwright named Gabriel Marcel, saw our current times 70 years ago reflecting on the consequences of moral relativism:

“Simone de Beauvoir wrote…that crimes against the common law — crimes, that is, against person or property — ought not to be judged with too much severity; but that political crimes…are inexpiable. Such an assertion, as soon as one reflects on it a little, opens out gulfs beneath one’s feet; it can be properly understood only if one lays bare the dogmatic philosophy of history which it presupposes. If political crime is a moral sin, the reason must be that it goes against the meaning of history and that the latter, of course, is supposed to be generally known."

That’s scary!

It keeps us from going to extremes. It provides opportunity to “detoxify our disappointments…and … take the radioactivity out of our resentments”[^10]

Political correctness is not a moral system nor does it seem to be anchored in any. It seems to be a set of values (fairness, toleration, equality) “arbitrarily wrenched from their context in the whole and then swollen to madness in their isolation."[^12]

Yet, how can we be sensitive if we don’t sense? For a plight to be heard, for a perspective to be shared, for a demand for equality voiced, freedom to speak is presupposed.

I pick on Political Correctness for the contradiction of it’s creed, the speciousness of its pretended self-righteousness, and the stultifying effects on thinking, especially moral thinking.

PC is a degraded form of respect, in that it wields its power with social pressure rather than moral persuasion.

This is not to say that a person who respects another is “politically incorrect”. They will not do or say harm, but they’ll have a reason why they don’t, whereas the PC adherent only knows because he’s been told.

This is the great irony of PC: it makes moral judgments without a categorically right or wrong foundation but applies them on a categorical way.
