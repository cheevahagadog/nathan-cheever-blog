By Nathan Cheever

Respect. When someone says, “I ask that you respect my preferences/choice/decision/identity”, how are we to respond? Or perhaps more pointedly: “why won’t you respect my ?”. Perhaps the supposition is, if it’s someone’s choice, it ought to be respected, observed. But what does respect in this case mean? Is it an invitation or an injunction to show admiration and deference to something worthy of those feelings? OED: to treat or regard with deference, esteem, or honor; to feel or show respect for.

In another definition from OED: To uphold, maintain, refrain from violating (a right, privilege, law, decision, etc.). Also: to refrain from harassing or obstructing (a person); to treat with consideration in regard to a particular course of action, function, etc.

When used this way, as in the first two sentences, respect does not mean honor, admire, or appreciate. It means to refrain from disagreeing, objecting, or violating. That’s what it means. I ask that you uphold or maintain my position. In this “respect”, “respect” isn’t the same as “respect”. If we’re being asked or told to ‘respect’ i.e. agree with, uphold a position, or to refrain from debate, this is not the same as being asked to respect something as worthy of deference (though it might be), but the demand itself doesn’t supply the argument for the merit of real respect. We need to work around definitions.

“‘We need to respect other’s differences”. I’ve heard this before.
