Life in the Face

By Nathan Cheever

My first triathlon is now only a few weeks away. Actually it’s more of a biathlon for me since my friend Tyler is doing the swimming leg. When he finishes, I’ll bike 14 miles and run three. But the constant wind, snow, and rain of this interminable winter have made training outside challenging.

So last Thursday after work I headed over to the gym at my work building. I changed into my running shorts in the bathroom and then entered the gym. I was alone. Just me and two muted TVs playing over the treadmills. I turned off the middle TV and clicked on the channel guide for the TV in front of me. I hoped to find something sportsy to watch; something exciting enough to distract me from my dislike of running. When I saw in the little green box the word “NASCAR”, I thought, “Watching cars zoom around a track at over 100 mph might help me run faster!”. ENTER.

I put the remote down, pressed the speed button “6”, and my three-mile run began.

To my chagrin, when I glanced up at the TV, I learned that it was not a race, but some documentary of a drag racer. I know nothing about NASCAR and I’m fine keeping it that way. I’ve seen a few racers on a cereal box, and I’m familiar with the name Dale Earnhardt Jr. That is the extent of my NASCAR knowledge and interest. “Oh great,” I thought. I was already running and I knew that if I tried to change the channel I’d probably kill myself fumbling with the remote. I decided it wasn’t worth the risk, and resigned myself to 30 minutes of TV boredom.

But by my second lap, the program started to grab my attention. I read the delayed closed-caption blurbs with 50% attention (up from 10%). It was a documentary about Paula Murphy, a legend in women’s drag racing. I gathered that this “Woman on Wheels” was quite the record-buster. And while her accomplishments were impressive (like surviving a malfunction in a hydrogen-peroxide-powered rocket dragster going over 250 mph!) what struck me was the expression on her face in her photos. Her face beamed a love of racing.

A thought hit me then. Paula Murphy did not have to choose racing, setting records, or risking her life for a sport. No doubt she had her doubters as a woman racing in the 1950s and 1960s. She sought out a challenging and risky course in life, both on and off the track. Society, friends, and family were not beating down her door telling her she had to race. Every time I saw her face in those photos I saw a person who was basking in the warmth of a feeling of summiting, of overcoming man and machine.

Her face seemed to say to me, “I didn’t care about staying in my lane, about what anyone thought, or what was considered safe or practical. I had to race!”.

I was now about halfway through my run. I’m also 35, halfway to 70 years old! I wondered, “When was the last time I had that look on my face?” Probably too long.

I upped my speed to 8 mph. I was reminded that that face, the face we all inwardly hope our family and friends will remember us by, is not the face of forced smiles for photos or the zoned-out face of passive smart-phone scrolling. Our best face comes from deep within and has an irrepressible beauty and contentment. These are the smiles that you can’t hold back. The preceding faces of discomfort, decision, and determination dress and push the face of our best and highest self out on stage for the world to see.

That “feeling” we all want deep down is not something comfort and stasis can provide. It’s over on the other side of difficulty. It must be earned.

Paula Murphy is in her nineties and will probably never read this. My race is nothing compared to her races. But thanks to her inspiration, I ran my fastest three-miles ever that day (with an average minute/mile of 8:25). Seeing all those pictures of her after her races made me think of my race, coming up in a few weeks. I can’t wait for the feeling of finishing after giving my best. Thanks for your smile Paula.
