What's up with Up?

In which I wonder about the significance of verticality.

By Nathan Cheever

The meaning of lines

A horizontal line is sensed as “being at rest, just as the human body is at rest when horizontal. They give a sense of stability, balance, and tranquility.”1

A vertical line is sensed as “one of aspiration, reaching, assertiveness”2. Instead of the restful equilibrium you see with horizontal lines, we get a dynamic equilibrium. There are forces held in check, just like a human body standing takes lots of muscles to keep it upright, or the strength of a tree’s roots holding the tree against the wind.

Verticality takes strength and effort to achieve. Perhaps that’s why “[they] have been used throughout history to commemorate significant events and establish particular points in space.”3

For example:
This is known as a Menhir, meaning “long stone” or “standing stone”. Thousands of years ago, vertical stones like these dotted the landscape across Europe, Africa, and Asia. Rune stones, Stonehenge, and even the great stone heads on Easter Island come to mind.

Steles and obelisks too, have been vertical line structures commemorating victories, achievements, and religious symbolism.

Gravestones too have inherited the obelisk at times. Here is Beethoven’s grave with the obelisk.

In Gothic cathedrals like Choir of Saint-Pierre, Beauvais, France, vertical lines dominate the visual experience. Think about everything in this picture as intentional, not necessary. I imagine the builders didn’t have to have so many vertical lines in the stones, but they did. This suggests “ascent, lift, weightlessness, aspiration, and a visual denial of tremendous forces.”4

Structures like this aren’t gotten cheaply. Erecting a large stone without the aid of our modern technology must haven taken a lot of effort not to mention leadership. Imagine the speech the shaman of a tribe living on the British Isles 5000 years ago had to give:

“Hey I have a wonderful idea! I know we need to gather food and build shelters, but here’s what let’s do: let’s go get that huge rock lying over there and stick it in the ground pointing up over here! Sure it will expend huge amounts of precious energy and time and probably crush a few of you, but won’t it be wonderful…to have it pointing upwards!?”

Joking aside, these intentional and labor intensive vertical buildings say a lot about our humanity and our ancestors understanding of value.

Church steeples, bell towers, minaret towers, altars… all use the vertical line to establish a particular point in space as special. They’re not designed for more ‘practical’ purposes, like shelter or providing food. There’s no obvious temporal benefit to a Stonehenge, but our ancestors, to astonishingly degrees of similarity, sought ways to point towards and connect to a higher realm. I think edifices like these are trying to point that out – that there’s a there up there.

Verticality at play in everyday language

This might seem a little “woo-woo”, that there’s a qualitative symbolistic metaphysics at work within how we see the vertical plane. I thought so too, until I realized that this qualitative realm along the vertical axis (up being better than down) has completely infiltrated our daily language and how we unconsciously think.

Take a look at the categories synonyms of the for the word “low”:

  • living in, coming from poor circumstances (poor, base, lowly)
  • close to the ground, short (below, depressed, flat)
  • reduced, mediocre (cheap, insignificant, inferior)
  • depressed (bad, down, dejected, downcast)
  • not feeling well (faint, reduced, weak)
  • crude, vulgar (base, rough, degraded)
  • not loud (muffled, subdued, hushed)

Contrast that with the synonyms of “high”:

  • extreme (great, rich, strong, precious)
  • very happy (joyful, merry, exhilarated)
  • strong on the senses (sharp, acute, piercing)
  • tall, at a great distance aloft (big, great, soaring)
  • important (significant, essential, noble, exalted)

And I think that that’s totally rad! Vertical thinking means thinking about our values; thinking about the _qualities of things.
